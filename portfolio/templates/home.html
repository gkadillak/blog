Garrett Kadillak

The journey into software started with Hollywood. Social Network, specifically. All of this talk about algorithms and being 'plugged in' and hacking were all completely alien to me. I had no way to grasp the reality that these characters being portrayed lived in. I came out of the movie with a burning curiousity. I desperately wanted to find out more about that environment.

This curiousity culminated in an online Java course via Udemy. The soft spoken instructors walked through the syntax of the language, object oriented programming, and a brief foray into algorithms. I would stay up late to code during school nights and I would rush to get my regular work done so I could have free time to advance further in the class. Needless to say, I quickly exhausted the introductory course and I was dismayed. I was simply wandering in the software sphere, unsure of where to head next. The courses that were best advertised were web development courses. So, I took up JavaScript (jQuery specifically) along with HTML5 and some basics of digital design.

I was doing exactly what I wanted to do! However, my enthusiasm was waning. I couldn't answer the simple question of how to utilize these skills, how to deploy a website, how to work in a team. There were so many things I didn't know I didn't know. So, I put it down coding for a while. Even though I tried to forget about the things I learned and forced myself to face the reality that I was in school to be a different brand of engineering - mechanical. However, there was always that nagging in the back of my head. What if you could do this full time? What if you got your questions answered and then some? What would that look like?

I was fortunate enough to be given a second chance to hone my skills durin my senior year at Colorado State University. I was given the opportunity to work on a user interface for the EcoCAR challenge. The most tell tale sign of my journey (thus far) was after our professor pitched each one of the senior design projects, my peers unanimously dubbed the user interface project their least favorite project. I was destined for software at this point - I just didn't know it.

I graduated college and found out that there were these courses that you could take that would give you all of the essential skills you needed in order to become a developer. It couldn't have come at a better time. I had been working at a small startup wandering and wondering what I would do. Now there was a clear direction which way to go. So I purchased a one way ticket to San Francisco and stayed with my gracious sister on her couch for 3 months.

The bootcamp was life changing and challenging. I came out of it with life long friends along with the skills to start a career. I was also fortunate enough to be able to work at the bootcamp after I graduated. Being a mentor to budding developers was its own experience that I would do all over again if given the chance. Concepts that I thought I knew were exposed as misunderstood as my own knowledge grew.

After the bootcamp experience ended, I moved on to being a core contributor for an open source project called Rockstor. I had worked with the founder at the bootcamp and his idea was fascinating. Open source took time to adjust to but he was a patient, understading mentor and helped guide me along until I met my next opportunity.

My time at FlightCar once again took adjusting as it was a more robust engineering team with dedicated groups. The team there was an excellent fit for me as I learned about agile development, production level Django, as well as working with a mobile team.
